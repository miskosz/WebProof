Vandermonde Matrix

For given numbers $a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}$, a Vandermonde matrix is an $n \times n$ matrix defined by \[ V = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & a_0 & a_0^2 & \dots & a_0^{n-1} \\ 1 & a_1 & a_1^2 & \dots & a_1^{n-1} \\ && \vdots && \\ 1 & a_{n-1} & a_{n-1}^2 & \dots & a_{n-1}^{n-1} \end{pmatrix}. \] I stumbled upon this wonderful proof that $V$ is regular if and only if $a_1,\dots,a_n$ are pairwise distinct.

Clearly, if two $a_i$ are the same, the matrix has two equal rows and therefore is singular. Let us suppose that they are distinct; we will show that $V$ is regular.

Let $f(x) = c_0 + c_1x + \dots + c_{n-1}x^{n-1}$ be a polynomial of degree smaller than $n$. Then \[ V \ \begin{pmatrix} c_0 \\ c_1 \\ \vdots \\ c_{n-1} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} f(a_0) \\ f(a_1) \\ \vdots \\ f(a_{n-1}) \end{pmatrix}. \]
That is just a straightforward matrix multiplication.

Suppose that $c = (c_0,\dots,c_{n-1})$ is a vector such that $Vc^T = 0$. Then the polynomial $f(x)$ with coefficients $c_i$ has $n$ distinct roots $a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}$. But it is of degree smaller than $n$, therefore it must be the zero polynomial. So every $c_i$ is zero, and $V$ is regular.
